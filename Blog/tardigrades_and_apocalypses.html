On Tardigrades and Apocalypses : Why this Cute Microbe is the Ultimate Survivor

  • Tardigrade hardiness borders on “indestructible”, having survived in environments most lifeforms wouldn’t.
  • To further prove this, some researchers have shed light on possible global apocalyptic events that tardigrades can weather.

Microbes are easily the most basic of all organisms. Their simplicity enables their survival almost anywhere on Earth . This makes them capable of providing crucial support mechanisms for lifeforms and ecosystems. For instance, there are trillions of microbes living in the average human body that facilitate normal body functions. Sadly, this pervasiveness can also disrupt life, causing diseases and death.

Granted the simplicity and popularity of microbes, some invisible critters showcase surprisingly superior self-preservation instincts. These microbes, known as extremophiles, exhibit extraordinary adaptation to extreme environments.

However, scientists have named one critter as a contender worthy of the title "survivor extrodinaire": the mighty tardigrade.

Tardigrades are found virtually everywhere. In boiling water. In freezing temperatures. In deserts and on mountains. They’ve even been on the moon. They can dive to the deepest ocean trenches and even go decades without food and water. But they much prefer living on wet mosses and lichens, thus earning them the name moss piglets.

Fact: There are about 1300 known species of tardigrades.

Tardigrades often withstand inhospitable conditions many animals wouldn’t. They can weather temperature drops of up to -272° Celsius and highs of up to 150° Celsius. Their barrel-shaped bodies can take six times the crushing pressure at the bottom of any ocean. They have even been to outer space and borne the extreme radiation found there.

This hardiness is partly due to a DNA protecting mechanism facilitated by a protein in their bodies called a “Damage suppressor”, a.k.a the Dsup. This protein, only found in tardigrades, keeps off harmful radiation from damaging their DNA.

Tardigrade survival is also aided by cryptobiosis. This is a state of dormancy and inactivity triggered by uncomfortably dry conditions.  They achieve this state by curling up into a ball known as a tun, a process involving squeezing out all the water from their bodies.

Fact: The body length of different tardigrade species vary between 0.3 to 1.2 mm.

While in cryptobiosis, tardigrades can wait for conditions to improve. When it does, they uncurl themselves and carry on as before.

To test just how robust tardigrades can be, some researchers looked into three possible extinction scenarios. These were apocalyptic‐scale doomsday events triggered by possible astrophysical phenomena. Naturally, such events would generate so much heat on the Earth that all oceans would dry and all water vapor would disappear from the Earth’s atmosphere.

We don’t know how life starts on a planet, said Dr. David Sloan, one of the researchers in the study, but since we’ve seen mass extinctions on Earth, we wanted to know if there are any astrophysical events that could kill off all life on a planet once it gets started.

The three possible events considered could be set in motion by a large impacting asteroid, a supernova, and gamma-ray bursts.  But why these events exactly?

  • Scientific evidence shows that about 65 million years past, a large asteroid struck the Earth and triggered a mass extinction. Presently, there are fifteen similar objects orbiting the sun. When given enough mass a similar event could be initiated. An earth-bound hit would eventually boil all the oceans.

    Note: Luckily, none of the monitored objects are on a collission course with the Earth at the moment.

  • When a massive star has reached its death, it explodes in a highly energetic event known as a supernova. This event releases radiation with the highest energies at light-speed. If that energy were to dry up the Earth’s oceans, a massive star would have to explode about 0.41 light-years away (about  3.88 × 1012 km) away.

    Note: Fortunately, Proxima Centauri, the closest star to our Sun, is not massive enough to set off a supernova. The nearest massive star likely to go supernova is only about 147 light-years (1.4 × 105 km) away.

  • Very energetic supernova events release what are called gamma-ray bursts. These bursts involve a type of radiation known as gamma radiation characterized by massive amounts of energy. They would have to originate from a distance of about 45 light-years (4.3 × 1014 km) for them to wipe out all the oceans from the Earth’s surface. 

    Note: Lucky for us earthlings, there is 1 chance in a billion a gamma-ray burst could happen that close.

In all three cases, tardigrades were more likely to survive getting roasted alive on Earth than not.

Fact: A light-year is the distance light travels in a year.

Could this mean that life can exist in different conditions from that on Earth?

In this context there is a real case for looking for life on Mars and in other areas of the Solar System in general, said Dr. Rafael Alves Batista, a co-author of the study from Oxford University. If tardigrades are Earth’s most resilient species, who knows what else is out there.

However, the tardigrade is not entirely death-defiant. In the next few billion years, the sun will swell into what we call a red giant, swallowing the entire Earth in the process. At that temperature, no life on Earth is expected to survive.
